# NIST AI Risk Management Framework (AI RMF)
The NIST AI Risk Management Framework (AI RMF 1.0) is a voluntary framework to help organizations manage risks across the AI lifecycle. It is organized around four core functions: GOVERN (cultivate a culture of risk management and policies), MAP (establish context and identify risks), MEASURE (analyze, assess, and track AI risks using metrics), and MANAGE (prioritize and act on risks, including monitoring and response). The framework emphasizes building trustworthy AI that is valid and reliable, safe, secure and resilient, accountable and transparent, explainable and interpretable, privacy-enhanced, and fair with harmful bias managed. In cybersecurity, the AI RMF is used to evaluate AI-enabled security tools and to govern the responsible deployment of AI systems.

# NIST Generative AI Profile
The NIST AI RMF Generative AI Profile (NIST-AI-600-1) is a companion resource that tailors the AI RMF to the unique risks of generative AI. It catalogs risks such as confabulation (hallucination), dangerous or harmful content, data privacy leakage, information integrity (disinformation, deepfakes), and the production of harmful code or CBRN information. For each, it suggests actions mapped to the GOVERN, MAP, MEASURE, and MANAGE functions. It also stresses content provenance and labeling of AI-generated content. Security teams use it to assess generative-AI features before adoption.

# MITRE ATLAS
MITRE ATLAS (Adversarial Threat Landscape for Artificial-Intelligence Systems) is a knowledge base of adversary tactics and techniques against machine-learning and AI systems, modeled after the well-known MITRE ATT&CK framework. ATLAS documents real-world attacks across tactics such as reconnaissance, ML model access, ML attack staging, and impact, including techniques like model evasion, data poisoning, model extraction (theft), membership inference, and prompt injection. It includes case studies of attacks on production AI systems. Defenders use ATLAS to threat-model AI systems, plan red-team exercises, and map mitigations to known adversary behavior.

# OWASP Top 10 for LLM Applications
The OWASP Top 10 for Large Language Model Applications lists the most critical security risks specific to LLM-powered software. The categories include: Prompt Injection (manipulating the model via crafted input), Insecure Output Handling (trusting model output without validation), Training Data Poisoning, Model Denial of Service, Supply Chain Vulnerabilities, Sensitive Information Disclosure, Insecure Plugin/Tool Design, Excessive Agency (giving the model too much autonomy or permission), Overreliance (trusting wrong answers), and Model Theft. It is the primary checklist for securing chatbots, RAG systems, and AI agents, and pairs naturally with secure-by-design development.

# OWASP Top 10 (Web Application Security)
The OWASP Top 10 is the long-standing awareness document for the most critical web-application security risks. Recent categories include Broken Access Control, Cryptographic Failures, Injection (including SQL injection and cross-site scripting), Insecure Design, Security Misconfiguration, Vulnerable and Outdated Components, Identification and Authentication Failures, Software and Data Integrity Failures, Security Logging and Monitoring Failures, and Server-Side Request Forgery (SSRF). It guides secure coding, code review, and penetration testing of web applications, and underpins the vibe-coding and pen-testing work later in this course.

# Google Secure AI Framework (SAIF)
Google's Secure AI Framework (SAIF) is a conceptual framework for securing AI systems built on six core elements: expand strong security foundations to the AI ecosystem, extend detection and response to bring AI into an organization's threat universe, automate defenses to keep pace with threats, harmonize platform-level controls for consistent security, adapt controls to adjust mitigations and create faster feedback loops, and contextualize AI system risks in surrounding business processes. SAIF helps organizations apply familiar security engineering practices to novel AI risks such as data poisoning, prompt injection, and model theft.

# Coalition for Secure AI (CoSAI)
The Coalition for Secure AI (CoSAI) is an open ecosystem and OASIS-hosted project of industry partners working to standardize approaches to AI security. Its workstreams address software supply-chain security for AI systems, preparing defenders for the changing AI threat landscape, and AI security governance. CoSAI publishes a risk map and shared guidance so that organizations can secure AI systems with common, interoperable controls rather than ad hoc approaches. It complements frameworks like NIST AI RMF and Google SAIF.

# EU AI Act
The EU Artificial Intelligence Act is the first comprehensive, risk-based legal framework for AI. It classifies systems into prohibited practices, high-risk systems (subject to strict obligations on risk management, data governance, transparency, human oversight, and robustness), limited-risk systems (transparency duties, such as labeling AI-generated content and deepfakes), and minimal-risk systems. Penalties for violations can reach tens of millions of euros or a percentage of global turnover. Security and compliance teams track the Act's phased deadlines when deploying AI in or for the EU market.

# MAESTRO Threat Modeling Framework
MAESTRO (Multi-Agent Environment, Security, Threat, Risk, and Outcome) is a threat-modeling framework, described in Omar Santos's book, tailored to agentic AI systems. It extends classic threat modeling (like STRIDE) to the layered architecture of AI agents — the foundation model, data and memory, tools and APIs, orchestration, and the multi-agent environment — and reasons about threats and mitigations at each layer. It is useful for systematically analyzing where an autonomous agent or multi-agent workflow could be attacked or misbehave, and for designing controls such as guardrails, policy gates, and human-in-the-loop checkpoints.

# Securing Agentic AI and the OWASP Top 10 for Agentic Applications
As AI systems gain autonomy and the ability to call tools, new risks emerge beyond single-prompt LLM use. Key concerns include excessive agency (an agent taking high-impact actions without approval), tool misuse and manipulation, memory poisoning, identity and authorization gaps between agents, insecure agent-to-agent communication, and weak observability or audit logging. Emerging guidance — including OWASP's work on agentic applications and the MAESTRO framework — recommends least-privilege tool access, policy gates and allowlists, human-in-the-loop approval for high-risk actions, strong authentication between agents, and thorough logging so autonomous workflows remain accountable and auditable.
